Page 5 | Confidential — For AI Agent Training Only  1. Alberta Debt Landscape & Legislative Framework 
Alberta operates within a dual federal-provincial framework for debt law. Federal legislation 
governs insolvency proceedings, while provincial legislation governs debt collection, civil 
enforcement, and consumer protection. Understanding both layers is essential for providing 
comprehensive debt advice to Albertans. 
1.1 Key Federal Legislation 
• Bankruptcy and Insolvency Act (BIA): The primary federal statute governing personal 
bankruptcy, consumer proposals, Division I proposals, and surplus income rules. 
Administered by the Office of the Superintendent of Bankruptcy (OSB) through Licensed 
Insolvency Trustees (LITs). 
• Criminal Code, s. 347 (Criminal Interest Rate): Effective January 1, 2025, the criminal 
interest rate was lowered from ~60% effective annual rate to 35% APR. Payday loans 
are separately capped at $14 per $100 borrowed federally. 
• Canada Student Loans Act / Canada Student Financial Assistance Act: Governs 
federal student loan programs, repayment assistance (RAP), and the 7-year discharge 
rule in bankruptcy. 
• Income Tax Act: Gives CRA broad collection powers including garnishment without a 
court order, set-off of refunds/benefits, and liens on property. 
1.2 Key Alberta Legislation 
• Civil Enforcement Act (CEA): Governs wage garnishment rules, exempt property in 
seizure proceedings, and enforcement of civil judgments in Alberta. Key source for asset 
exemption amounts. 
• Limitations Act (RSA 2000, c. L-12): Sets the 2-year limitation period for creditors to 
sue on debts, plus a 10-year ultimate limitation. Acknowledgment or partial payment can 
reset the 2-year clock. 
• Consumer Protection Act / Fair Trading Act: Regulates debt collection agencies, debt 
repayment agencies, and payday lenders. All must be licensed with the Province of 
Alberta. 
• Collection and Debt Repayment Practices Regulation: Detailed rules governing how 
collectors can contact debtors, prohibited practices, and complaint processes. 
• Payday Loans Regulation: Regulates short-term lending including maximum loan 
amounts ($1,500), terms (42–62 days), and borrowing costs.

Page 7 | Confidential — For AI Agent Training Only  2. Types of Debt & Priority Rules 
2.1 Secured vs. Unsecured Debt 
Secured debt is backed by collateral (e.g., mortgage on a home, lien on a vehicle). If the 
borrower defaults, the creditor can seize the collateral. Examples: mortgages, vehicle loans, 
home equity lines of credit (HELOC). 
Unsecured debt has no collateral backing. The creditor must obtain a court judgment before 
seizing assets or garnishing wages. Examples: credit cards, personal loans, medical bills, utility 
bills, most student loans. 
2.2 Priority Debts 
Some debts take priority over others in insolvency and cannot easily be eliminated: 
• Child and spousal support arrears: Survive bankruptcy; cannot be discharged. MEP 
can garnish wages without a court order. 
• Court-ordered fines and restitution: Not discharged in bankruptcy. 
• Debts arising from fraud or misrepresentation: Not discharged if creditor can prove 
fraud. 
• Student loans (within 7 years): Government student loans are not dischargeable if 
bankruptcy occurs within 7 years of ceasing to be a student (5-year hardship exception 
available). 
• CRA tax debt: Can be included in consumer proposals and bankruptcy, but CRA has 
special collection powers and may oppose discharge if tax debt is large (≥$200,000 and 
≥75% of total unsecured claims).

Page 8 | Confidential — For AI Agent Training Only  3. Debt Collection Rules in Alberta 
3.1 Licensing Requirements 
All collection agencies, collectors, debt repayment agencies, and debt repayment agents must 
be licensed under Alberta’s Consumer Protection Act and the Collection and Debt Repayment 
Practices Regulation. The following are NOT covered by these rules: original creditors collecting 
their own debts, lawyers collecting debts for clients, and civil enforcement bailiffs seizing 
property under a court order. 
3.2 What Collectors Cannot Do 
• Contact frequency: Cannot make more than 3 unsolicited contacts in any 7-
consecutive-day period (excludes mail and mistaken contacts). 
• Hours: Cannot contact you before 7:00 AM or after 10:00 PM Alberta time. 
• Workplace: Cannot contact you at work if you have asked them not to and provided 
alternative contact arrangements. 
• Third parties: Cannot discuss your debt with anyone except you (unless that person 
has accepted responsibility or you gave permission). 
• Threats/harassment: Cannot use threatening, profane, intimidating, or coercive 
language. 
• False information: Cannot produce, distribute, or publish false, misleading, or 
deceptive information. 
• Costs to debtor: Cannot contact you in a way that costs you money (e.g., collect calls). 
• Old debts: Cannot pursue a non-judgment debt where the last payment or written 
acknowledgment is more than 6 years old. 
• Police involvement: Cannot involve or threaten to involve the police in debt collection 
matters. 
• Suggest others are liable: Cannot suggest a spouse, friend, or relative is responsible 
for your debt unless they have accepted responsibility. 
3.3 What Collectors Must Do 
• Identify themselves, the name of the collection agency, the creditor they represent, and 
the amount owed. 
• Send a written collection notice to the debtor’s last-known address before further 
contact. 
• Maintain records of all collection activities for at least 3 years. 
• Prove the debt exists if challenged by the debtor. 
3.4 Filing a Complaint 
If a collection agency violates these rules, the debtor can file a consumer complaint with Service 
Alberta’s Consumer Investigations Unit. Service Alberta can investigate and take enforcement 
action including fines or licence revocation. Contact: 780-427-4088 or toll-free 1-877-427-4088.

4.1 The Two-Year / Ten-Year Rule 
Under Alberta’s Limitations Act (RSA 2000, c. L-12), a creditor must commence legal action 
within: 
• 2 years after they first knew or ought to have known about the debt (the “discoverability” 
clock); AND 
• 10 years after the claim originally arose (the “ultimate” limitation period). 
Whichever expires first applies. After these periods, the debt becomes “statute-barred” — 
meaning a creditor can no longer obtain a court judgment to enforce it. 
4.2 Resetting the Clock 
IMPORTANT: The 2-year limitation period can be reset (restarted) if the debtor:  
• Acknowledges the debt in writing (an admission that the sum is due and unpaid). 
• Makes a partial payment toward the debt. 
Even casual written communications (email, text, DM) admitting the debt can reset the clock. 
Once the 10-year ultimate limitation has expired, however, the debt is permanently statute-
barred and cannot be reset by any action. 
4.3 What Statute-Barred Means (and Doesn’t Mean) 
• Does mean: The creditor cannot sue you or obtain a judgment. No wage garnishment or 
asset seizure is possible through the courts. 
• Does NOT mean: The debt disappears. You still technically owe it. Collectors can still 
call (though they cannot threaten legal action). The debt may remain on your credit 
report for up to 6 years from date of last activity.

Page 10 | Confidential — For AI Agent Training Only  5. Wage Garnishment in Alberta 
5.1 How Garnishment Works 
Most creditors must first sue the debtor and obtain a court judgment before issuing a garnishee 
summons to the debtor’s employer. Exceptions to the court-order requirement include: 
• Canada Revenue Agency (CRA): Can garnish wages without a court order for unpaid 
taxes or overpaid benefits. 
• Maintenance Enforcement Program (MEP): Can garnish wages for unpaid 
child/spousal support without a court order. 
• Credit unions: Exempt from the court order requirement under certain circumstances. 
• Federal student loans in default: CRA may garnish wages once the loan is in 
collections. 
5.2 Alberta Garnishment Exemptions (Civil Enforcement Act) 
Garnishment exemptions are calculated on NET monthly income (after taxes and mandatory 
deductions): 
Income Tier  Garnishment Rule  Effect 
First $800/month  100% exempt  Cannot be touched  
$800 to $2,400/month  50% can be garnished  You keep at least half  
Above $2,400/month  100% can be garnished  No protection above this tier  
Per dependent  +$200 to exempt amount  Increases $800 base by $200 per 
dependent  
 
Example: A single person earning $3,000/month net. First $800 is exempt. Of the next $1,600 
($800–$2,400), 50% = $800 can be garnished. Remaining $600 above $2,400 is fully 
garnishable. Total garnishment: $800 + $600 = $1,400/month. 
5.3 Exempt Income Types 
• Canada Pension Plan (CPP) benefits — generally exempt from regular creditor 
garnishment 
• Employment Insurance (EI) benefits 
• Workers’ Compensation Board (WCB) payments 
• Social assistance / AISH (Alberta Income for the Severely Handicapped) 
• Widow’s pension, handicap benefits — if kept separate from other funds 
IMPORTANT: Self-employed individuals do NOT benefit from the $800 base exemption. 
Creditors can potentially garnish 100% of self-employed business income.  
5.4 Stopping Wage Garnishment 
• Negotiate with creditors: Propose a voluntary payment plan before or after 
garnishment begins. 
• Orderly Payment of Debts (OPD): Filing triggers a stay of proceedings that stops 
garnishment immediately.

in bankruptcy. Exemptions apply to the EQUITY in an asset (value minus secured debt), and

6.2 Farming Exemptions 
• Farmland: Up to 160 acres exempt if it includes your principal residence and is part of 
your farming operation. 
• Farm equipment & supplies: All personal property necessary to conduct farming 
operations for the next 12 months is exempt. 
6.3 What Happens If Equity Exceeds the Exemption? 
If you have equity above the exempt amount, you have two options in bankruptcy: (1) pay the 
trustee the difference between your equity and the exemption, allowing you to keep the asset; or 
(2) surrender the asset to the trustee for sale, with the exempt portion returned to you. 
Example: Home worth $350,000 with a $300,000 mortgage = $50,000 equity. The exemption is 
$40,000. You would need to pay $10,000 to the estate to keep the home, or explore a consumer 
proposal as an alternative.

Page 13 | Confidential — For AI Agent Training Only  7. Consumer Proposals 
7.1 Overview 
A consumer proposal is a legally binding agreement between a debtor and their unsecured 
creditors, administered by a Licensed Insolvency Trustee (LIT) under the BIA. It allows the 
debtor to repay a portion of their debts over time, typically at a significant reduction. 
7.2 Eligibility 
• Maximum debt: $250,000 in total unsecured debts (excluding mortgage on principal 
residence). For debts above $250,000, a Division I Proposal is available. 
• Insolvent: Must be unable to pay debts as they come due, or owe more than the fair 
value of assets. 
• Only a LIT can file: Only a Licensed Insolvency Trustee can administer a consumer 
proposal. Beware of debt relief companies that charge fees and then refer to a LIT. 
7.3 Process 
• 1. Consultation with a LIT — free initial assessment of income, debts, assets, and 
expenses. 
• 2. LIT drafts a proposal offering creditors more than they would receive in a bankruptcy. 
• 3. Proposal is filed with the OSB — an automatic stay of proceedings takes effect 
immediately. 
• 4. Creditors have 45 days to accept or reject (a meeting of creditors may be called). 
• 5. Majority in dollar value must vote to accept for the proposal to be binding on ALL 
unsecured creditors. 
• 6. Debtor makes fixed monthly payments to the LIT for up to 5 years (can pay off early 
with no penalty). 
• 7. LIT distributes payments to creditors. Upon completion, remaining debts are 
discharged. 
7.4 Key Features 
Feature Details

8.1 Overview 
Personal bankruptcy is a legal process under the BIA that provides an honest but unfortunate 
debtor with a fresh financial start. The debtor assigns their non-exempt assets to a Licensed 
Insolvency Trustee, who converts them to cash and distributes proceeds to creditors. Upon 
discharge, most unsecured debts are eliminated. 
8.2 Eligibility 
• Must owe at least $1,000 in total debts. 
• Must be insolvent (unable to pay debts as they come due, or total debts exceed fair 
value of assets). 
• Must reside in, carry on business in, or have property in Canada. 
8.3 Surplus Income Rules (2025) 
The OSB sets income thresholds based on household size. If your net monthly income exceeds 
the threshold by more than $200, you must pay 50% of the surplus to the estate, and your 
bankruptcy is extended.

• Surrender all non-exempt assets and credit cards to the LIT.

• Debts for dividends a director is liable to pay to a corporation.

Page 17 | Confidential — For AI Agent Training Only  9. Orderly Payment of Debts (OPD) — Alberta-Specific 
9.1 Overview 
The Orderly Payment of Debts (OPD) program is a unique debt consolidation option available in 
Alberta (also Saskatchewan, PEI, and Nova Scotia). It is legislated under Part X of the BIA and 
administered exclusively in Alberta by Money Mentors, a non-profit organization. OPD 
consolidates unsecured debts into a single monthly payment at a fixed 5% interest rate. 
9.2 How It Works 
• A provincial credit counsellor (Money Mentors) files an application to the Court for a 
consolidation order. 
• The court order consolidates your unsecured debts into one monthly payment. 
• You pay 100% of your unsecured debt + 5% interest, distributed to creditors by Money 
Mentors. 
• Maximum repayment period: up to 5 years (some sources cite 3 years as typical; the BIA 
allows up to 5). 
• A stay of proceedings stops wage garnishment, collection calls, and lawsuits. 
9.3 Eligibility 
• Must be an Alberta resident. 
• Must have a stable source of income. 
• Must be unable to pay debts as they come due. 
• Only unsecured debts are included — CRA tax debts and business debts are excluded. 
• Creditors owed more than $1,000 must consent to be included.

Page 18 | Confidential — For AI Agent Training Only  10. Credit Counselling & Debt Management Programs 
10.1 Non-Profit Credit Counselling 
Non-profit credit counselling agencies provide free or low-cost financial education, budgeting 
assistance, and debt management programs. Key Alberta/national organizations include: 
• Money Mentors (Alberta): Non-profit, exclusive OPD provider, offers free financial 
counselling to all Albertans. 
• Credit Counselling Society: National non-profit offering free debt counselling and 
DMPs across Canada. 
• Credit Counselling Canada: National association of non-profit credit counselling 
organizations. 
10.2 Debt Management Programs (DMPs) 
A DMP is a voluntary agreement between the debtor and creditors, administered by a credit 
counselling agency. The agency negotiates lower interest rates and consolidates payments into 
a single monthly amount. Key differences from OPD: DMPs are not court-ordered and not 
binding on creditors; creditors can withdraw at any time. Typical term: 3–5 years. 
10.3 Warning: For-Profit Debt Relief Companies 
IMPORTANT: Many for-profit debt settlement companies market aggressively in Alberta, 
charging thousands in fees before referring clients to a LIT. Only a Licensed Insolvency Trustee 
can file a consumer proposal or bankruptcy. Non-profit credit counsellors (e.g., Money Mentors, 
Credit Counselling Society) provide free referrals. The OSB maintains a searchable directory of 
licensed trustees.

Page 20 | Confidential — For AI Agent Training Only  12. Student Loan Debt 
12.1 The 7-Year Rule 
Government student loans (Canada Student Loans, provincial student loans) receive special 
treatment in insolvency. Under s. 178(1)(g) of the BIA, these loans are NOT automatically 
discharged in bankruptcy or consumer proposal unless the bankruptcy/proposal occurs more 
than 7 years after the debtor ceased to be a full-time or part-time student. 
12.2 Key Rules (Updated After Piekut v. Canada, 2025 SCC 13) 
• Single-date approach confirmed: The Supreme Court of Canada (April 2025) 
confirmed the “single-date” approach — the 7-year clock starts from the LAST date you 
ceased to be any kind of student (full-time or part-time), not from each separate 
program. 
• End-of-study date: The later of: the last day of the month you stopped attending school, 
OR the last day of the month you completed final exams for your last semester. 
• 5-year hardship exception: After 5 years, a court MAY order discharge if the debtor 
acted in good faith AND will continue to experience significant financial difficulty. 
Requires a court application (not automatic). 
• Private student loans: Bank-issued student lines of credit, student credit cards, and 
other private student debt are NOT subject to the 7-year rule. They are treated like any 
other unsecured debt and can be discharged at any time. 
12.3 Repayment Assistance Program (RAP) 
Before considering insolvency, students struggling with payments should explore the 
Repayment Assistance Program (RAP): 
• RAP reduces or eliminates monthly payments based on income and family size. 
• Payments are adjusted every 6 months through reapplication. 
• After 15 years of repayment (or 10 years for borrowers with permanent disabilities), the 
remaining balance may be forgiven. 
• Being on RAP does NOT negatively affect your credit score — your loan remains in 
good standing. 
• As of 2024, federal student loans are interest-free; only some provincial loans still 
charge interest. 
12.4 Default and CRA Collections 
If a borrower misses payments for approximately 9 months (270 days), the loan goes into 
default and is transferred to CRA for collection. CRA can: garnish wages and bank accounts 
without a court order, withhold tax refunds and benefit payments (CCB, GST credit), and 
register liens. To bring a defaulted loan back into good standing: pay outstanding interest + 
make the equivalent of 4 monthly payments, then contact the NSLSC to complete the process.

13.1 CRA’s Special Collection Powers 
CRA has significantly broader collection powers than ordinary creditors: 
• No court order required: CRA can garnish wages, freeze bank accounts, and seize 
assets without first obtaining a court judgment. 
• Wage garnishment: CRA is not bound by the standard $800 exemption. CRA can 
garnish up to 50% of employment income, and 100% of amounts owed to independent 
contractors. 
• Bank account freeze: CRA can issue a Requirement to Pay (RTP) to your bank, 
freezing and seizing funds. 
• Set-off: CRA can intercept tax refunds, GST/HST credits, CCB payments, and other 
federal benefits to apply against tax debt. 
• Property liens: CRA can register a lien (memorial) against your property at the Land 
Titles Office. 
• Director liability: Directors of corporations can be personally liable for unremitted 
GST/HST, payroll deductions (CPP, EI, income tax). 
13.2 Dealing with CRA Debt 
• Payment arrangements: CRA may agree to a payment plan, but interest continues to 
accrue. Call the CRA collections line to negotiate. 
• Taxpayer relief (fairness) provisions: CRA can cancel or waive penalties and interest 
in cases of extraordinary circumstances (financial hardship, illness, disaster). Must apply 
within 10 years. 
• Consumer proposal: CRA tax debt CAN be included in a consumer proposal. This is 
one of the key advantages of proposals over OPD (which cannot include CRA debt). 
• Bankruptcy: CRA tax debt CAN be discharged in bankruptcy. However, if tax debt is 
$200,000+ and represents 75%+ of total unsecured claims, a court hearing is required 
for discharge. 
13.3 CRA Limitation Period 
CRA generally has a 10-year limitation period to collect assessed tax debt (from the date of the 
Notice of Assessment or Reassessment). The limitation can be extended by certain actions 
such as acknowledgment, partial payment, or filing an objection/appeal. Unlike provincial 
limitation periods, CRA’s collection power is governed by federal legislation (Income Tax Act).

Effective January 1, 2025, the criminal interest rate under s. 347 of the Criminal Code was

Page 23 | Confidential — For AI Agent Training Only  15. Family Debt Issues in Alberta 
15.1 Joint Debt 
When two people are co-borrowers or co-signers on a debt, each is liable for the FULL amount. 
If one person files for bankruptcy or a consumer proposal, the other remains fully liable. The 
creditor can pursue the non-filing co-signer for the entire debt. 
15.2 Spousal Liability 
In Alberta, spouses (married or adult interdependent partners) are NOT automatically liable for 
each other’s debts unless they: 
• Co-signed or guaranteed the debt. 
• Are joint account holders. 
• Are jointly named on credit applications. 
Supplementary credit card holders are generally NOT liable for the primary cardholder’s debt 
(check the specific cardholder agreement). 
15.3 Debt and Separation/Divorce 
• Joint debts: A separation agreement or court order allocating debt between spouses 
does NOT release either party from joint debts in the eyes of the creditor. The creditor 
can still pursue either party for the full amount. 
• Matrimonial property division: Under Alberta’s Family Property Act, both assets AND 
debts are divided. Net family property includes debts incurred during the relationship. 
• Support obligations: Child and spousal support cannot be discharged in bankruptcy or 
consumer proposal. Support arrears survive insolvency. 
15.4 Death and Debt 
When a person dies in Alberta, their estate (not their surviving spouse or family) is responsible 
for repaying debts. Exceptions: 
• Joint debts: the surviving co-borrower becomes solely responsible. 
• Guaranteed debts: the guarantor becomes responsible. 
• Life insurance with named beneficiaries: bypasses the estate and is NOT available to 
creditors. 
• Registered accounts (RRSP, TFSA, RRIF) with named beneficiaries: pass outside the 
estate. 
If the estate has insufficient assets to pay all debts, creditors cannot pursue surviving family 
members for the shortfall (unless they co-signed).

16.1 Debt Repayment Methods 
Avalanche method: Pay minimum on all debts; direct extra payments to the debt with the 
HIGHEST interest rate. Mathematically optimal — saves the most money in interest over time. 
Snowball method: Pay minimum on all debts; direct extra payments to the SMALLEST balance 
first. Provides psychological momentum through quick wins. May cost more in interest but 
improves motivation and compliance. 
Hybrid approach: Pay off one small debt first for momentum, then switch to the avalanche 
method for the remaining debts. 
16.2 Budgeting Frameworks 
• 50/30/20 Rule: 50% of after-tax income to needs, 30% to wants, 20% to savings and 
debt repayment. 
• Zero-based budgeting: Every dollar is assigned a purpose; income minus expenses 
equals zero. Good for people who need strict spending control. 
• Pay-yourself-first: Automate savings/debt payments immediately when paid, then live 
on the remainder. 
16.3 Emergency Fund 
Before aggressively paying down debt, build a small emergency fund ($500–$1,500) to prevent 
new debt from unexpected expenses. Once high-interest debt is eliminated, build the 
emergency fund to 3–6 months of essential expenses. 
16.4 Negotiation Tactics 
• Lump-sum settlements: Many creditors (especially collection agencies) will accept a 
lump sum of 30–70% of the outstanding balance. Always get the settlement agreement 
in writing before paying. 
• Interest rate reduction: Call your credit card company and request a lower rate. If 
you’ve been a good customer, they may reduce the rate to retain you. 
• Hardship programs: Many banks offer temporary hardship programs that reduce or 
defer payments for 3–6 months.
