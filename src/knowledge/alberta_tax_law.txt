This document provides comprehensive reference material for an AI agent to achieve expert-level proficiency 
in Alberta personal and family tax law. It covers the dual federal-provincial tax system, all current rates, credits, 
deductions, family-specific rules, registered savings plans, estate planning, and strategic planning

1.1 Canada's Dual Tax System 
Canada operates a dual income tax system. Personal income tax is levied at both the federal level (by the 
Canada Revenue Agency, or CRA) and the provincial level. Alberta residents pay both federal income tax and 
Alberta provincial income tax. The CRA administers both on behalf of Alberta, meaning Albertans file a single 
tax return that calculates both federal and provincial tax. 
Alberta is unique among Canadian provinces for having no provincial sales tax (PST) and no harmonized sales 
tax (HST). Only the federal 5% Goods and Services Tax (GST) applies. This is a significant tax advantage 
compared to other provinces. 
Alberta also has among the lowest probate fees in Canada (maximum $525), no provincial payroll tax, and no 
provincial health premium, making it one of the most tax-friendly provinces for individuals and families. 
1.2 Key Governing Legislation 
• Income Tax Act (Canada) — Federal statute governing all income taxation 
• Alberta Personal Income Tax Act — Provincial statute setting Alberta-specific rates and credits 
• Canada Pension Plan Act — Governs CPP contributions and sharing 
• Employment Insurance Act — Governs EI premiums 
• Family Law Act (Alberta) — Governs non-divorce family matters including support 
• Divorce Act (Canada) — Federal statute for divorce proceedings 
• Wills and Succession Act (Alberta) — Governs estates and wills 
• Surrogate Rules (Alberta) — Governs probate fees and process 
• Federal Child Support Guidelines and Alberta Child Support Guidelines 
• Spousal Support Advisory Guidelines (SSAG) — Non-binding but widely used 
1.3 Key Administrative Bodies 
• Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) — Administers both federal and Alberta income tax, benefits 
• Alberta Tax and Revenue Administration (TRA) — Administers corporate tax, fuel tax, tourism levy 
• Alberta Maintenance Enforcement Program (MEP) — Enforces child and spousal support orders 
• Alberta Child Support Recalculation Program — Annual income-based recalculation of child support

CREDITS 
3.1 Basic Personal Amount (BPA) 
Level 2025 2026 
Federal BPA  $16,129 $16,452 
Alberta BPA  $22,323 $22,769 
The BPA is the amount of income an individual can earn without paying any tax. The federal enhanced BPA is 
income-tested: it is reduced for individuals with net income above $177,882 (2025) and fully eliminated at 
$253,414. Alberta's BPA is not income-tested. 
The Alberta BPA credit is calculated at 8% (the lowest provincial rate). The federal BPA credit is 14% (for 
2026) of the BPA amount. 
3.2 Key Alberta Non-Refundable Tax Credits (2025) 
• Spousal/Common-Law Partner Amount: Up to $22,323 (reduced by spouse's net income) 
• Eligible Dependant Amount (equivalent-to-spouse): Up to $22,323 
• Alberta Age Amount (age 65+): Up to $6,221 (reduced when net income exceeds $46,308, eliminated at 
$87,782) 
• Pension Income Amount: Up to $1,719 
• Disability Amount: Mirrors federal DTC certificate requirement 
• Caregiver Amount for Infirm Dependants Age 18+: Up to $12,922 
• Medical Expenses: Same eligible expenses as federal; excess over lesser of 3% of net income or threshold 
• Tuition Tax Credit: 8% of eligible tuition fees 
• Adoption Expenses: Up to $19,354 per child 
• Charitable Donations: 8% on first $200, then varying rates above 
• Political Contributions: Alberta political contribution tax credit 
• Alberta Supplemental Tax Credit: 2% of non-refundable credits exceeding $60,000 (new for 2025) 
3.3 Spousal and Equivalent-to-Spouse Credits 
The spousal amount allows an individual to claim a credit for supporting a spouse or common-law partner with 
low or no income. The maximum claim is the BPA minus the dependent spouse's net income. A common-law 
partner is someone who has lived with you in a conjugal relationship for at least 12 continuous months, or is 
the parent of your child. 
The equivalent-to-spouse (eligible dependant) credit is available to single, separated, divorced, or widowed 
taxpayers who support a dependant in their home. This can be a child under 18, or a parent/grandparent, or 
any relative who is dependent due to mental or physical infirmity. Only one equivalent-to-spouse claim can be 
made per household. 
3.4 Canada Workers Benefit (CWB) 
The CWB is a refundable tax credit for low-income working individuals and families. For 2025, single 
individuals with working income above $3,000 can receive the benefit, with the maximum single benefit of 
approximately $1,518. Families can receive up to $2,616. The benefit phases out at higher income levels. 
Alberta residents use the federal CWB calculation (no separate Alberta working income credit).

Page 6 4. CAPITAL GAINS TAXATION 
4.1 Inclusion Rate — Current Status (as of February 2026) 
The capital gains inclusion rate remains at 50% for all taxpayers. The proposed increase to 66.67% (two-thirds) 
— originally announced in the 2024 Federal Budget for June 25, 2024, then deferred to January 1, 2026 — 
was formally cancelled by Prime Minister Carney on March 21, 2025, prior to the 2025 federal election. 
Current rule: 50% of net capital gains are included in taxable income for individuals, corporations, and trusts. 
Principal Residence Exemption: Fully maintained. Capital gains on a principal residence are entirely tax-free. 
Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE): $1,250,000 for qualified small business corporation shares and 
qualified farming/fishing property (increased from $1,016,836 effective June 25, 2024). Indexed for inflation 
starting 2026. 
4.2 Property Flipping Rules 
If you sell residential property within 365 days of purchase and do not meet an exemption condition, the gain is 
treated as fully taxable business income (100% inclusion, not 50%) and the principal residence exemption does 
not apply. Exemptions include death, disability, separation/divorce, threat to personal safety, employment 
relocation, and insolvency. 
4.3 Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT) 
The AMT was substantially revised effective January 1, 2024. The federal AMT rate increased from 15% to 
20.5%. The basic exemption increased to the start of the fourth federal bracket ($177,882 for 2025, indexed). 
Capital gains are included at 100% for AMT purposes (up from 80%). This makes large capital gains events 
more susceptible to triggering AMT. AMT paid in excess of regular tax carries forward for seven years. 
Provincial AMT applies whenever federal AMT applies. Graduated Rate Estates are exempt from AMT.

Contributions are tax-deductible and reduce taxable income. Investment growth is tax-deferred. Withdrawals

Spousal RRSP: A higher-income spouse can contribute to a spousal RRSP (within their own contribution

Lifelong Learning Plan (LLP): Withdraw up to $20,000 for full-time education. Each spouse can withdraw 
separately.

Parameter  2025 2026 
Annual Contribution Limit  $7,000 $7,000 
Cumulative Room (if eligible since 2009)  $102,000  $109,000  
Contributions are not tax-deductible, but all investment growth and withdrawals are completely tax-free. 
Withdrawals are added back to contribution room the following January 1. No attribution rules on TFSA 
contributions: a higher-earning spouse can gift money to a lower-earning spouse for TFSA contributions 
without any income attribution concerns. 
TFSA income and withdrawals do not affect income-tested benefits (CCB, GST/HST credit, OAS, GIS, ACFB). 
This makes TFSAs especially powerful for families receiving these benefits.

Lifetime Contribution Limit: $50,000 per beneficiary (no annual limit) 
Canada Education Savings Grant (CESG): Federal government matches 20% of annual contributions, up to

Canada Learning Bond (CLB): Up to $2,000 per child for low-income families; no personal contribution

Canada Disability Savings Bond (CDSB): Up to $1,000/year for low-income beneficiaries (no contribution 
required) 
Eligibility: Must be eligible for the Disability Tax Credit (approved Form T2201) 
Contributions are not deductible. Growth is tax-sheltered. Withdrawals include a taxable portion (grants, bonds, 
and growth). Qualifying family member rules extended to end of 2026, allowing siblings to be qualifying family 
members.

6.1 Canada Child Benefit (CCB) 
The CCB is a tax-free monthly payment from the federal government to eligible families with children under 18. 
It is income-tested based on adjusted family net income (AFNI) from the prior year's tax return. 
Child Age  Max Annual Amount (July 2025 –June 2026)  
Under 6 $7,997 ($666.41/month)  
Ages 6–17  $6,748 ($562.33/month)  
The CCB is reduced at higher income levels. For families with AFNI above $36,502 (2025), the reduction 
begins at varying rates depending on the number of children. For AFNI above $79,087, additional phase-out 
applies. 
Shared Custody: When both parents have the child approximately equally (40%–60% of the time), the CRA 
splits the CCB, calculating each parent's entitlement separately based on their individual AFNI. 
Filing Requirement: Both parents must file tax returns to receive CCB. Late filing delays payments. 
6.2 Alberta Child and Family Benefit (ACFB) 
The ACFB is Alberta's provincial tax-free benefit for low- and middle-income families with children under 18. It 
is administered by the CRA on Alberta's behalf and paid quarterly (August, November, February, May). It 
replaced the former Alberta Child Benefit and Alberta Family Employment Tax Credit in July 2020. 
6.2.1 Base Component (July 2025–June 2026) 
• 1 child: up to $1,330/year 
• 2 children: up to $1,995/year 
• 3 children: up to $2,527/year 
• 4+ children: up to $2,923/year 
The base component is available regardless of employment income. It is reduced when adjusted family net 
income exceeds $27,565 and phases out completely at $46,191. 
6.2.2 Working Component (July 2025–June 2026) 
• 1 child: up to $718/year 
• 2 children: up to $1,718/year 
• 3 children: up to $2,218/year 
• 4+ children: up to $2,468/year 
Available to families with working income exceeding $2,760. The benefit increases at 15% of each additional 
dollar above $2,760 until the maximum is reached, then reduces when AFNI exceeds $46,191. 
Automatic Enrollment: No application needed. If you qualify for the federal CCB and file your tax return, you 
are automatically assessed for the ACFB. 
ACFB is non-taxable and does not affect AISH, Income Support, or Alberta child care subsidy eligibility. 
6.3 GST/HST Credit 
A quarterly tax-free payment for individuals and families with low to modest income. For the July 2025 to June 
2026 period, the maximum annual amount is approximately $519 for a single individual, $680 for a couple, plus 
$179 per child under 19. The credit phases out at higher income levels.

Page 11 7. INCOME SPLITTING STRATEGIES 
7.1 Pension Income Splitting (ITA s.60.03) 
A pensioner may allocate up to 50% of eligible pension income to their spouse or common-law partner through 
an annual joint election using Form T1032. This is NOT automatic — both spouses must file the T1032 with 
matching amounts. Failure to file invalidates the split. 
Under 65: Only life annuity payments from a registered pension plan (RPP) qualify. 
Age 65+: RRIF withdrawals, RPP payments, life annuity payments from an RRSP, and certain other pension 
income all qualify. 
CPP Sharing: CPP has its own separate sharing mechanism under the Canada Pension Plan Act. You must 
apply separately; it is NOT done through the T1032 pension splitting election. 
Pension splitting can reduce overall family tax, preserve OAS entitlement (by keeping individual net income 
below the OAS clawback threshold of $95,323 for 2026), and allow both spouses to claim the $2,000 pension 
income tax credit. 
7.2 Spousal RRSP Contributions 
A higher-income spouse contributes to a spousal RRSP (using the contributor's room) to build retirement 
savings taxed at the lower-income spouse's rate upon withdrawal. The three-year attribution rule requires that 
the annuitant spouse not withdraw from any spousal RRSP for three calendar years after the last contribution; 
otherwise the withdrawal is taxed in the contributor's hands. 
7.3 TFSA Gifting 
A higher-earning spouse can gift money to the lower-earning spouse for TFSA contributions. Since TFSA 
attribution rules do not apply, all growth and withdrawals belong to the recipient spouse. This is the simplest 
legal income-splitting tool available. 
7.4 Tax on Split Income (TOSI) — "Kiddie Tax" 
TOSI rules (ITA s.120.4) tax certain types of income received by family members from a related business at the 
highest marginal rate. This includes dividends and capital gains from private corporations paid to adult family 
members who do not make a sufficient contribution to the business (the "excluded amount" test). Children 
under 18 are subject to TOSI on virtually all split income. These rules significantly limit family income splitting 
through private corporations. 
7.5 Attribution Rules 
When a taxpayer transfers or lends property to a spouse or common-law partner, income earned on that 
property (interest, dividends, rental income) is attributed back to the transferor and taxed in their hands. Capital 
gains from transfers to a spouse are also attributed back. However, these rules can be avoided by: lending at 
the CRA prescribed rate (currently significant), transferring at fair market value with proper consideration, or by 
contributing to the spouse's TFSA. 
When property is transferred or lent to a minor child (under 18), income (but not capital gains) is attributed back 
to the transferor. Capital gains earned by the minor on transferred/loaned property are NOT attributed back and 
are taxed in the child's hands.

Page 12 8. SEPARATION AND DIVORCE TAX IMPLICATIONS 
8.1 Marital Status for Tax Purposes 
Your marital status on December 31 determines filing status for that tax year. If you separated during the year 
and remained separated for at least 90 consecutive days (ending in the tax year), you are considered 
"separated" as of the date of separation. This affects credit eligibility, benefit calculations, and filing 
requirements. 
8.2 Spousal Support (Alimony) 
Deductible for Payer / Taxable for Recipient IF: Payments are periodic (regular intervals), made under a 
written agreement or court order, paid directly to the recipient, and made while living separate and apart due to 
relationship breakdown. 
NOT Deductible/Taxable: Lump-sum payments (unless arrears of periodic support), payments to third parties 
for specific expenses (unless specifically included in the court order), and voluntary payments without a written 
agreement. 
Structuring spousal support for tax efficiency can benefit both parties. The payer gets a deduction reducing 
their taxable income; the recipient includes it as income but may be in a lower tax bracket, resulting in less total 
tax paid by the family unit. 
8.3 Child Support 
CRITICAL RULE: Child support payments made under agreements or court orders dated after May 1, 1997 
are NOT deductible by the payer and NOT taxable to the recipient. 
The Divorce Act prioritizes child support over spousal support. Where there are insufficient funds for both, child 
support is paid first. 
Child support is calculated using the Federal Child Support Guidelines based on the paying parent's income 
(line 15000) and the number of children. Additional "Section 7 expenses" (childcare, medical, educational, 
extracurricular) are shared proportionate to income. 
8.4 Property Transfers on Separation 
Property transferred between spouses as part of a separation agreement or court order is generally transferred 
at the adjusted cost base (tax-deferred rollover). No immediate capital gains tax is triggered. The receiving 
spouse inherits the original cost base and will realize the gain or loss upon eventual disposition. 
⚠ IMPORTANT: If you sell the matrimonial home, the principal residence exemption can eliminate capital 
gains. However, if either spouse owned other properties designated as principal residence for overlapping 
years, careful planning is needed.  
8.5 RRSP/Pension Division on Separation 
RRSPs and pension assets can be divided between separated spouses on a tax-deferred basis under a court 
order or written separation agreement (ITA s.146(16) for RRSPs, Pension Benefits Standards Act for 
pensions). The transfer does not trigger immediate tax. The receiving spouse will pay tax upon withdrawal. 
8.6 Canada Child Benefit After Separation 
The primary caregiver receives the full CCB amount based on their individual AFNI. In shared custody 
(approximately equal parenting time), CRA splits the benefit and calculates each parent's entitlement based on 
their individual income. Both parents must notify CRA of the separation and custody arrangement.

have lived together in a relationship of interdependence for at least 3 continuous years, or who have a 
relationship of some permanence if they have a child together. For tax purposes, the CRA uses the federal 
definition of common-law partner (12 months cohabitation or parent of your child). These partners have the 
same tax rights and obligations as married spouses for all federal and provincial tax purposes.

Page 14 9. ESTATE PLANNING AND TAXATION AT DEATH 
9.1 No Estate or Inheritance Tax in Canada 
Canada does not levy a formal estate tax, inheritance tax, or death tax. However, significant taxes can arise at 
death through deemed dispositions and income inclusion. The practical effect can be substantial. 
9.2 Deemed Disposition at Death 
At death, the deceased is deemed to have disposed of all capital property at fair market value (FMV) 
immediately before death. This triggers capital gains on appreciated assets including investment portfolios, 
rental properties, vacation homes, and private company shares. The gains are reported on the deceased's 
terminal (final) T1 return for the year of death. 
9.3 RRSP/RRIF at Death 
The full fair market value of RRSPs and RRIFs is included in the deceased's income on the terminal return, 
unless transferred to a qualifying beneficiary (spouse, financially dependent child or grandchild, or infirm child 
of any age). Spousal rollover: if the spouse is named as beneficiary, the RRSP/RRIF rolls over tax-free to the 
surviving spouse's RRSP/RRIF. 
9.4 TFSA at Death 
The TFSA value at date of death is tax-free. A successor holder (spouse/common-law partner) can take over 
the TFSA without tax implications. If there is no successor holder and the TFSA passes through the estate, 
income earned AFTER the date of death is taxable. 
9.5 Principal Residence Exemption 
The PRE remains available at death, eliminating capital gains on the deceased's principal residence. Only one 
property per family unit can be designated as a principal residence for any given year. 
9.6 Spousal Rollover 
Capital property transferred to a surviving spouse or common-law partner (or qualifying spousal trust) is 
automatically rolled over at the adjusted cost base, deferring all capital gains until the surviving spouse dies or 
disposes of the property. No election is required — it is the default treatment. 
9.7 Alberta Probate Fees 
Alberta has among the lowest probate fees in Canada. They are based on the NET value of the estate (unlike 
most provinces which use gross value):

9.9 Estate Planning Tools 
• Beneficiary designations on RRSPs, TFSAs, RRIFs, and life insurance bypass the estate (avoids probate)

• Life insurance provides tax-free death benefit to cover tax liabilities at death

Page 16 10. SENIORS-SPECIFIC TAX PROVISIONS 
10.1 Age Amount 
Available to individuals aged 65+ at December 31. For 2025: federal amount up to $8,790 (reduced when net 
income exceeds $44,325); Alberta amount up to $6,221 (reduced when net income exceeds $46,308, 
eliminated at $87,782). 
10.2 Pension Income Amount 
Both federal and Alberta provide a tax credit on up to $2,000 of eligible pension income ($1,719 Alberta for 
2025). Eligible income includes RPP payments, RRIF withdrawals (if age 65+), life annuity from RRSP, and 
certain other pension income. Pension splitting allows the receiving spouse to also claim this credit. 
10.3 OAS Clawback 
Old Age Security payments are subject to a recovery tax (clawback) when individual net income exceeds 
$95,323 (2026). The clawback rate is 15 cents per dollar above the threshold. OAS is fully eliminated at 
approximately $157,000 of net income. Pension splitting, RRSP contributions (before 71), and TFSA usage can 
help keep net income below the threshold. 
10.4 Alberta Seniors Benefit 
A provincial tax-free monthly payment for low-income seniors aged 65+ who receive federal OAS. For 2025, 
maximum annual benefit is approximately $3,868 for a single senior with zero non-deductible income. The 
benefit varies by income, accommodation type, and marital status. Application is required. Couples' income is 
split 50:50 for calculation purposes. 
10.5 Seniors Property Tax Deferral Program 
Alberta allows eligible seniors to defer paying all or part of their property taxes for up to 10 years. A lien is 
placed on the property. This can provide cash flow relief during retirement without triggering tax consequences. 
10.6 Medical Expenses 
Seniors often have significant medical expenses. The medical expense tax credit allows claiming expenses 
above the lesser of 3% of net income or the threshold ($2,635 federally for 2025). Claiming a 12-month period 
ending in the current tax year provides flexibility for timing. In the year of death, the period extends to 24 
months.

Page 17 11. SELF-EMPLOYMENT AND SPECIAL SITUATIONS 
11.1 Self-Employment Income 
Self-employed individuals report net business income (revenue minus expenses) on their tax return. Both the 
federal and Alberta tax brackets apply. Self-employed individuals pay both the employee and employer 
portions of CPP (total 11.9% for 2025 on earnings between $3,500 and $71,300). Half of the self-employed 
CPP contribution is deductible from income. 
Self-employed individuals and their spouses have an extended tax filing deadline of June 15, but any taxes 
owing are still due April 30. 
11.2 Rental Income 
Rental income is reported at the individual level and taxed at marginal rates. Allowable deductions include 
mortgage interest (not principal), property taxes, insurance, repairs, maintenance, utilities (if included), 
advertising, property management, and capital cost allowance (depreciation). CCA claimed on rental property 
reduces the cost base, increasing eventual capital gains. 
11.3 Investment Income Types and Tax Treatment 
• Interest income: Fully taxable at marginal rate (100% inclusion) 
• Eligible dividends (from public companies): Grossed up by 38%, then a federal dividend tax credit of 
15.02% and Alberta credit of 8.12% apply. Effective rate is significantly lower than on regular income. 
• Non-eligible dividends (from small businesses/CCPCs): Grossed up by 15%, then smaller dividend tax 
credits apply. 
• Capital gains: 50% inclusion rate (current). Taxed at half the marginal rate. 
• Return of capital: Not immediately taxable but reduces adjusted cost base, increasing future capital gains. 
11.4 Foreign Income 
Canadian residents are taxed on worldwide income. Foreign tax credits are available to prevent double taxation 
on foreign-source income. Both federal and provincial (Alberta) foreign tax credits can be claimed for taxes 
paid to foreign governments.

14.1 Filing Deadlines

14.2 Payment Requirements 
Taxes owing must be paid by April 30 to avoid interest charges. If you owe more than $3,000 in the current

15, June 15, September 15, December 15). 
14.3 Penalties 
• Late filing penalty: 5% of balance owing plus 1% per month (max 12 months) 
• Repeated late filing: 10% plus 2% per month (max 20 months) 
• RRSP over-contribution: 1% per month on excess above $2,000 buffer 
• TFSA over-contribution: 1% per month on excess amount 
• FHSA over-contribution: 1% per month on excess amount 
14.4 Alberta-Specific Filing Notes 
Alberta's personal income tax is calculated on Form AB428, completed as part of the federal T1 return. Alberta 
has no separate provincial filing requirement. All Alberta credits and deductions are claimed through the single 
CRA filing process.

ensure timely benefit payments.
